Embeddings turn complex objects into vectors so that geometry becomes meaningful. Words, documents, users, products, and images can all be mapped into a shared vector space where nearby points correspond to similar roles or meanings. This makes vector spaces a practical language for retrieval and recommendation, not just an abstract math topic.

One point of view is relational structure. An embedding is useful when distances and directions encode something interpretable about the task. If two items co-occur in similar contexts or produce similar downstream behavior, the representation should place them near one another.

A second point of view is approximation. The vector does not capture every detail of the original object; it compresses what the model thinks matters. That is why evaluating an embedding often means checking whether the geometry preserves the right distinctions rather than demanding a perfect reconstruction.

The conceptual payoff is that linear algebra becomes operational. Dot products, norms, and matrix multiplications are no longer isolated textbook procedures; they become the mechanics behind search, ranking, clustering, and representation learning systems.
